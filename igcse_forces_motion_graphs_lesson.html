Key rules summary
Distance-time graphs

A distance-time graph shows how far an object has travelled from its starting point over time. The gradient (slope) of the line tells you the speed of the object.

Speed = distance ÷ time    →    gradient = Δd / Δt    (unit: m/s)

What each type of line means

Horizontal line (zero gradient) → object is stationary
Straight diagonal line → constant speed (steeper = faster)
Curved line getting steeper → object is accelerating
Curved line getting shallower → object is decelerating

Worked example

Question: A car travels 120 m in 8 s at constant speed, then stops for 4 s, then travels 60 m in 6 s. Calculate the speed during each moving section.

Section A (0–8 s): speed = 120 m ÷ 8 s = 15 m/s
Section B (8–12 s): gradient = 0, so speed = 0 m/s (stationary)
Section C (12–18 s): speed = 60 m ÷ 6 s = 10 m/s
Note: Section C is slower — the line is less steep.

Curved lines and acceleration

On a distance-time graph, if the line curves, the object is not at constant speed. To find the speed at a specific point on a curve, draw a tangent at that point and calculate its gradient.

Speed at a point = gradient of tangent at that point
Velocity-time graphs

A velocity-time graph shows how an object's velocity changes over time. Two key things you can read from it:

Gradient of line
= Acceleration (m/s²)
Area under graph
= Distance travelled (m)
Acceleration = change in velocity ÷ time    →    a = Δv / Δt

What each type of line means

Horizontal line → constant velocity (zero acceleration)
Line sloping up → acceleration (velocity increasing)
Line sloping down → deceleration (velocity decreasing)
Curved line → non-uniform acceleration (gradient changing)

Worked example — acceleration and distance

Question: A train accelerates from rest to 30 m/s in 10 s, travels at 30 m/s for 20 s, then decelerates to rest in 15 s. Find: (a) acceleration, (b) deceleration, (c) total distance.

(a) Acceleration (0–10 s): a = (30−0) ÷ 10 = 3 m/s²
(b) Deceleration (30–45 s): a = (0−30) ÷ 15 = −2 m/s² (magnitude 2 m/s²)
(c) Total distance = area under graph:
Triangle A (0–10 s): ½ × 10 × 30 = 150 m
Rectangle B (10–30 s): 20 × 30 = 600 m
Triangle C (30–45 s): ½ × 15 × 30 = 225 m
Total = 150 + 600 + 225 = 975 m

Area under graph — shapes to use

Shape of sectionFormulaWhen to use it
Rectanglewidth × heightConstant velocity
Triangle½ × base × heightUniform acceleration from/to zero
Trapezium½ × (a + b) × hUniform acceleration, not from zero
Key rules — both graph types
Distance-time: gradient
Gradient = speed. Steeper slope = faster. Horizontal = stationary. Can never slope downward (distance can't decrease).
Distance-time: curve
Curve getting steeper = accelerating. Curve getting shallower = decelerating. Speed at a point = gradient of tangent.
Velocity-time: gradient
Gradient = acceleration. Positive slope = speeding up. Negative slope = slowing down. Horizontal = constant speed.
Velocity-time: area
Area under graph = distance travelled. Split into rectangles and triangles. Always use the area between the line and the time axis.
Units — always check
Speed/velocity: m/s. Acceleration: m/s². Distance: m. Time: s. Convert km/h → m/s by ÷ 3.6.
Velocity vs speed
Speed is scalar (just magnitude). Velocity is vector (magnitude + direction). VT graphs can go below zero — this means moving in the opposite direction.
